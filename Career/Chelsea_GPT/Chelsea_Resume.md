## [8:50](https://youtu.be/sGOiktiv6Oo?t=530) Series Introduction

Welcome to this new Q&A series focused on **building resumes that get you interviews**. Over the past 12 weeks we built your LinkedIn presence. Now we're turning that visibility into proof on paper.

- Think of **LinkedIn as your public brand**
- Your **resume is your sales sheet** — it highlights what convinces recruiters you can deliver

---

## [9:56](https://youtu.be/sGOiktiv6Oo?t=596) The Six-Second Scan

When a recruiter opens your resume, they spend **six to 10 seconds** on the first scan. Here's what they look for immediately:

- **The top third** of your resume
- **Job titles and company names**
- **Visible metrics or measurable outcomes**
- **Keywords that match the role**

### What Makes a Recruiter Stop Scrolling

- A **clear target role or headline** at the top
- **Bullets that show outcomes, not tasks**
- **Consistency** between your resume and LinkedIn profile

Recruiters aren't trying to read your resume. They're trying to **confirm a fit fast**. The goal is to make that confirmation easy.

---

## [13:30](https://youtu.be/sGOiktiv6Oo?t=810) Resume Headline Placement

Your resume needs a **headline** (target role line). Placement:

- Directly **underneath your name**
- Between your name and contact information
- Should reflect the **job title you're targeting** (e.g., "Full Stack Developer")

The headline should be **somewhat similar** to your LinkedIn headline — not identical, but using the same target language. If you're targeting full stack developer roles, that phrase should be **sprinkled throughout** your resume.

---

## [15:00](https://youtu.be/sGOiktiv6Oo?t=900) Job Titles: Remove "Junior"

Remove the word **"Junior"** from your job titles on your resume. If the program title was "Junior Developer," update it to reflect the actual work you're doing now (e.g., **"Full Stack Developer"**).

The intent of the program is for students to step into roles that are not junior-level. Your resume should reflect where you're headed, not where you started.

---

## [20:33](https://youtu.be/sGOiktiv6Oo?t=1233) Metrics and Measurable Outcomes

Recruiters look for **visible metrics or measurable outcomes** in your bullet points. Examples from a student resume:

- "Refactored legacy code for **80% greater readability** and maintainability"
- "Ensured quality with unit and integration tests, increasing project-wide coverage to **75%**"

**If you have data, use it.** When you don't have precise numbers, Dr. Emily's guidance applies: give your **best guess**. A recruiter won't be able to tell a number is a best estimate versus a precise measurement.

---

## [26:57](https://youtu.be/sGOiktiv6Oo?t=1617) The Bullet Formula: Action + Task + Result

Every bullet should follow this formula:

> **Action verb + Task + Result**

**Weak bullet:** "Worked on website updates."

**Strong bullet:** "Improved site load time by **40%** through optimized code deployment."

### The Key Mindset Shift

The difference between describing your work and **marketing your value**:

- **Describing:** "Helped with customer emails"
- **Marketing:** "Resolved **50+ support tickets per week**, reducing average response time from 24 hours to 6"

**Stop thinking about your resume as a description of the work you've done. It's a marketing tool.**

---

## [33:00](https://youtu.be/sGOiktiv6Oo?t=1980) Recruiter Workflow: What Happens After the Scan

The recruiter's process after scanning your resume:

1. **Scan** for keywords, measurable outcomes, and evidence the person knows what they're doing
2. **Pass** to the hiring manager with a note: "I liked this resume and this person enough to pass it to you"
3. The **hiring manager** evaluates technical details — that's their job, not the recruiter's

Recruiters work in close partnership with hiring managers. Some hiring managers want to see measurable outcomes; others will ask about them in the interview. They may ask: "Tell me about the work you did at TechJoy Software. What were the projects? What did the data show?"

---

## [38:00](https://youtu.be/sGOiktiv6Oo?t=2280) Pre-Production Projects: Showing Value Without Metrics

**The challenge:** Many TechJoy projects are real client work but don't have live users yet, so there's no production data to reference.

**Chelsea's guidance:** If the project doesn't have metrics, **focus on scope, scalability, intent, and technical impact** instead.

- You can show value **without fake numbers**
- Recruiters are looking for evidence that **on paper, you can do this job**
- They want to be excited enough to ask to speak with you

### What Recruiters Actually Assume

Recruiters assume that because you're working on **real projects with real clients**, there will be some measurable metrics. When there aren't:

- A recruiter won't know whether a metric is precise or estimated
- **But if there are no metrics at all, don't invent them** — find a creative way to describe what you did
- The hiring manager will understand the boot camp context: "Oh, they were working on real clients but didn't have production data yet. That's reasonable."

---

## [43:30](https://youtu.be/sGOiktiv6Oo?t=2610) Writing Bullets Without Data

When you can't quantify impact, focus on:

- **Scope** — what you built, what technologies you used
- **Scalability** — what the system was designed to handle
- **Intent** — what problem it was designed to solve
- **Technical impact** — code quality, architecture decisions

**Example pivot:** Instead of trying to claim "increased something by 100% because it didn't exist before," describe what you built and its purpose. If you created an entire admin dashboard from scratch, describe the technology, the integration, and the capability it provides.

For **support ticket** work, that data is easier to pull and quantify — use your best guess for those metrics.

---

## [55:25](https://youtu.be/sGOiktiv6Oo?t=3325) Spec Work and Creative Descriptions

The analogy: creative advertising graduates build **spec work** (speculative portfolio pieces) to demonstrate their abilities without real campaigns. Software developers in training face a similar challenge.

- You will need to be **very creative** in explaining what you've done and its validity
- The market is saturated — finding creative ways to describe your work will help you **get the recruiter's attention**
- Once you get the interview, you'll have ample opportunity to explain the context

---

## [59:49](https://youtu.be/sGOiktiv6Oo?t=3589) Homework Assignment

1. **Take one weak bullet** and rewrite it using the **Action + Task + Result** formula
2. **Refine the top third** of your resume
3. **Include a clear target role line** (headline)
4. **Make sure your LinkedIn headline and summary** match the same target language (similar, not identical)

### Key Takeaway

> **Visibility attracts attention, but proof earns interviews.**

Don't think that lacking data, percentages, or metrics on your resume will prevent recruiter interest. There is a creative way to talk about what you've done without quantifying it — focus on authentic, specific descriptions of your contributions.

## [12:34](https://youtu.be/odPIvVCLsYs?t=754) The Metrics Problem for Early-Career Developers

Chelsea spoke with **tech recruiter colleagues** about the concern that TechJoy students can't quantify their project impact. The key finding:

> **Your resume will NOT look unbelievable and will NOT prevent you from getting an interview if you don't list measurable results.**

If you don't list fabricated metrics, it will actually make you look **authentic** — as long as you focus on:

- **Evidence of process**
- **Scope**
- **As much outcome as you know** (not hard business metrics)

## [17:25](https://youtu.be/odPIvVCLsYs?t=1045) Impact Logic: Six Strategies for Credible Bullets

Tech recruiters recommended writing with **impact logic** — credible bullets when you can't quantify numbers.

### 1. Show Scope Instead of Data

- "Developed authentication flow for full-stack MERN app with **JWT and React Context**"
- "Built **6 REST API endpoints** to handle CRUD operations for product data"

This scope language shows **technical depth** without requiring user metrics.

### 2. Show Quality or Improvement Language

- "Improved app reliability by **refactoring frontend components** for reusable logic"
- "Enhanced code readability and **reduced redundancy** through modularization"

Recruiters recognize these as **measurable improvements even without percentages**.

### 3. Use Frequency or Scale When Exact Numbers Aren't Tracked

- "Debugged **multiple front-end issues weekly** in collaboration with QA team"
- "Implemented feature updates across **three sprint cycles** following agile methodology"

### 4. Emphasize Collaboration and Delivery

Show results in terms of **teamwork and contribution** rather than solo metrics.

### 5. Show Results in Terms of Learning or Contribution

Frame your work around **what you gained** and **what you delivered** to the project.

### 6. Use Project or Course Outcomes

**Boot camp or personal project outcomes still count.** Examples:

- "Completed **12-week coding boot camp** focused on full-stack JavaScript development"
- "Practiced version control, deployment, and testing in **real-time collaborative projects**"
- "Presented final capstone project to technical mentors and peers, explaining **architecture and decisions**"

**Optional soft skill bullet:** "Balanced multiple project deadlines while maintaining **code quality and documentation standards**"

# Resume Clinic Week 2: Structure and Format
Source: https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ

## [7:30](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=450) Session Overview

Tonight's focus is on **structure and format** — if a recruiter can't navigate your resume in six seconds, they won't. The goal is to understand the difference between resume structures, when to use each, and how formatting impacts a recruiter's readability.

Recruiters scan resumes within **6 to 10 seconds**. They look for:

- **Clarity**
- **Structure**
- **Alignment with the role**

The format should **guide their eyes, not overwhelm them**. There needs to be cohesive design sensibility — consistent font size, simple font choice, clean layout.

---

## [8:13](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=493) Two Core Resume Structures

### Reverse Chronological

- **Most common and preferred** for consistent career paths
- Best when you're staying in the same field you've been working in
- Highlights **steady progression** and recent experience
- Easy for recruiters and **applicant tracking systems (ATS)** to read

### Functional (Skills-Based)

- **Ideal for career changers** or those with mixed experience
- Groups experience **under skill categories instead of dates**
- Highlights **transferable skills and impact** instead of job titles
- Chelsea recommends this format for most TechJoy students since the majority are career shifters

---

## [16:30](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=990) When to Use Each Format

| Use Reverse Chronological When... | Use Functional When... |
|---|---|
| Staying in the same industry | **Pivoting careers** (e.g., teacher becoming developer) |
| Consistent experience and titles | Gaps or **mixed experiences** |
| Want to showcase steady growth | Need to highlight **skills before timeline** |

The functional resume works well for people with **multiple talents** that complement each other. A recruiter seeing diverse but related experience (e.g., lighting design + web development) gets insight into who you are and what your path looks like.

## [23:00](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=1380) Resume Anatomy: Non-Negotiable Sections

Every resume must include these sections:

1. **Header** — Name, LinkedIn profile link, contact information, GitHub link (for developers), portfolio link (if you have produced work you have permission to highlight)
2. **Summary** — Two to three sentences minimum; can go longer if warranted
3. **Core Skills / Technical Stack** — 100% non-negotiable
4. **Experience or Projects** — Must be present
5. **Education and Certifications** — List all certifications

---

## [25:12](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=1512) Formatting Rules

- **Keep margins balanced** and fonts consistent
- Font size can vary slightly — name and header elements can be a bit larger than body text
- **No photographs** on your resume (standard in Europe but not in the US; it's a distraction)
- **No design-heavy templates** — graphics, multiple colors, and decorative elements distract ATS systems and the AI that parses skills and competencies
- **Stick to one page** unless you have extensive relevant experience; two pages is acceptable, three is for very experienced professionals
- **Keep it simple** to ensure ATS systems read your resume correctly

---

## [26:50](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=1610) Skills Section Formatting

Two acceptable approaches for listing skills:

- **Bulleted list** of individual skills
- **Grouped by category** under the type of skill or technology

Either format works — choose what makes sense for the volume and type of skills you have. If you have many valuable skills, organizing them into columns or categories can keep things clean and readable.

## [30:00](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=1800) Live Resume Edit: Condensing the Header

Chelsea demonstrated live edits on a student resume in Google Docs. Key changes:

### Header Condensation

**Before:** Full URLs written out for LinkedIn, Portfolio, and GitHub
**After:** Hyperlinked text — just the word "LinkedIn," "Portfolio," or "GitHub" each linking to the URL

This saves significant space and looks cleaner.

### Headline Placement

The target role headline (e.g., "WordPress Developer") goes **directly underneath your name**, before contact information and summary.

### Spacing and Formatting

- Set line spacing to **single**
- Use **custom spacing** (before/after paragraph) set to approximately 3pt for consistency
- Only bold **section headers**, not body text
- Use consistent indentation for bullet points
- Move bullet points to align with the left margin using the ruler

These formatting adjustments **significantly reduced the resume's page count** without removing any content.

## [32:28](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=1948) Hyperlinks in Resumes

**Hyperlink your profile links** instead of writing out full URLs:

- The word "**LinkedIn**" hyperlinked to your LinkedIn profile
- The word "**Portfolio**" hyperlinked to your portfolio site
- The word "**GitHub**" hyperlinked to your GitHub profile

This keeps the header compact and professional.

## [46:30](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=2790) Using Google Docs for Resume Management

### Exporting for Applications

- **Download as PDF** when ready to submit
- **Never apply with a Word document** — it can be altered

Your resume is a **living, breathing story** of your professional career. It will constantly evolve, especially in tech where frameworks, databases, and tools change rapidly. Skills that are essential today may be obsolete in a year or two.

A clear, focused format shows recruiters you **respect their time** — it's the first sign you're ready to be hired.

## [9:01](https://youtu.be/sYWh4NN-mKU?t=541) Two Core Resume Structures

There are two core structures for resume formats. Which one you choose depends on your career path.

**Design clarity beats decoration.** The format should be nice, clean, readable, and easy on the eyes. This matters for both **ATS systems** and **recruiters**, who scan resumes in **6 to 10 seconds**. They look for clarity, structure, alignment with the role, and a format that guides their eyes without overwhelming them.

---

## [9:01](https://youtu.be/sYWh4NN-mKU?t=541) Reverse Chronological Resume

The **reverse chronological resume** is the most common and preferred structure for consistent career paths.

**Use when:**
- You are staying in the same industry or field
- You have consistent experience and titles
- You want to showcase steady progression and recent experience

**Advantages:**
- Most common and preferred by recruiters
- Highlights steady progression and recent experience
- Very easy for both recruiters and **ATS systems** to read

---

## [15:50](https://youtu.be/sYWh4NN-mKU?t=950) Functional Resume

The **functional resume** is skills-based and ideal for career changers or those with mixed experience.

**Use when:**
- You are pivoting careers or completely rebranding yourself
- You have gaps or mixed experience
- You want to highlight **transferable skills and impact** instead of job titles

**How it works:**
- Groups experience under **skill categories** instead of dates
- Highlights transferable skills and impact instead of job titles
- Often includes **core competencies**, skills, and project highlights
- Lists chronological work experience toward the bottom
- Tends to be shorter than reverse chronological resumes

**Key rule:** In a functional resume, you need to highlight **skills before the timeline**. Skills are what matters most.

---

## [27:38](https://youtu.be/sYWh4NN-mKU?t=1658) When to Use Each Format

| Situation | Recommended Format |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Staying in the same field | Reverse chronological |
| Consistent experience and titles | Reverse chronological |
| Pivoting careers | Functional |
| Gaps or mixed experience | Functional |
| Complete rebrand | Functional |
| Low-code to full-stack (adjacent shift) | Could go either way |

**Think about which structure fits your career path.** If you are staying in the same field, reverse chronological is where it belongs. If you are completely pivoting and rebranding yourself, use a functional resume.

---

## [33:45](https://youtu.be/sYWh4NN-mKU?t=2025) Resume Anatomy: The Non-Negotiables

These sections must appear on **both** functional and reverse chronological resumes:

1. **Name and header** with LinkedIn, contact information, and GitHub (if you are a developer)
2. **Portfolio link** (if you have produced projects you are proud of and have permission to showcase)
3. **Summary** -- minimum 2 sentences, max 5. Guidance is 2 to 3, but you can add a bit more. Not a story.
4. **Core skills or technical stack**
5. **Experience and/or projects**
6. **Education and certifications**

---

## [36:17](https://youtu.be/sYWh4NN-mKU?t=2177) Formatting Rules

- Keep **margins balanced** and **fonts consistent**
- **Line spacing** matters -- everything should be easy on the eyes
- Keep it **clean and polished**
- **Avoid design-heavy templates** that ATS systems cannot read
- **Do not put a headshot** on your resume (that is a European/Asian standard, not used in the U.S.)
- **Stick to one page** unless you have several years of experience
  - Two pages is acceptable
  - Three pages is excessive
- **Clarity matters more than decoration**

> If your resume can tell your story in one clean glance, you have done your job.

**Readability, alignment, simplicity** -- that is what recruiters look for.

A **clear, focused format** shows recruiters you respect their time and is the first sign you are ready to be hired.

## [6:29](https://youtu.be/Utec2BjFJrg?t=389) What Is a Professional Summary?

The **professional summary** is the about section of your resume. It should mirror your LinkedIn about section but **not say the exact same thing**.

Your resume summary tells the reader: **who you are, what you do, and what you want next** -- in 3 to 5 sentences. It must also show **impact** and give **proof** of who you are. No fluff.

---

## [12:43](https://youtu.be/Utec2BjFJrg?t=763) What a Summary Is and Is Not

**A summary is NOT:**
- An objective statement like "to obtain a role where I can grow"
- A generic list of soft skills
- A paragraph packed with buzzwords and no proof

**A professional summary IS:**
- A **short, focused snapshot** of your value
- Aligned to a **specific role or role family**
- Written in **language that recruiters use** in job descriptions
- Backed by **real work, projects, or skills**

In today's market, a recruiter might review hundreds of resumes for one role. If your summary sounds **generic**, they move on. If your summary sounds **targeted and confident**, they keep reading.

---

## [14:36](https://youtu.be/Utec2BjFJrg?t=876) Resume and LinkedIn Alignment

Everything in your resume summary has to line up with your LinkedIn about section. If your summary says one thing and your LinkedIn says something completely different, that **hurts trust**.

You want one **clear, consistent story** across both. But the resume summary should **not be identical** to the LinkedIn about section -- it should be different while mirroring the same narrative.

---

## [18:09](https://youtu.be/Utec2BjFJrg?t=1089) The 4-Line Summary Structure

Use this structure every time for your resume summary (3 to 5 sentences):

- **Line 1:** Who you are and your main focus
- **Line 2:** What you do well, including tools or skills
- **Line 3:** Proof of impact or contribution
- **Line 4/5:** What you want next and how you want to add value

# Resume Clinic Week 4: Using the Right Keywords

## [5:50](https://youtu.be/tUXJ2lTkoCs?t=350) Why Keywords Matter

Recruiters search for candidates the same way you search for things online: **keywords**. Keywords determine how you show up in recruiter searches. Your resume needs **three kinds of keywords**:

- **Technical skills** — languages, tools, frameworks (e.g., React, Node.js, SQL, REST APIs)
- **Role-specific terms** — front end, test automation, ETL, REST APIs
- **Soft skills tied to delivery** — collaboration, communication, agile, sprint planning

Hiring teams use these terms in job descriptions, recruiter searches, and interview criteria. **ATS systems scan for these keywords**, and recruiters skim for them quickly.

---

## [9:58](https://youtu.be/tUXJ2lTkoCs?t=598) Align Resume Keywords with LinkedIn

Your resume keywords should **match your LinkedIn skills**. That alignment increases search visibility and helps your profile appear in relevant job alerts.

---

## [10:48](https://youtu.be/tUXJ2lTkoCs?t=648) How to Extract Keywords from Job Descriptions

Walk through a real job description and pull keywords the way recruiters do. Look for:

- **Job title** — e.g., Lead Full Stack Engineer
- **Responsibilities** — what the role requires day-to-day
- **Required skills or qualifications** — must-have technical skills
- **Preferred skills** — nice-to-have additions
- **Tools mentioned in the stack** — specific technologies listed

**Priority rules:**
- Any keyword that appears **multiple times** is a priority
- Any keyword that appears in **both required and preferred** sections is a must-add

---

## [14:48](https://youtu.be/tUXJ2lTkoCs?t=888) Live Keyword Extraction Exercise

Pull a job description that matches your target role and identify:

1. **Top 3 technical keywords**
2. **Top 2 role/action keywords**
3. **Top 2 environment keywords**
4. **Any repeated tool or framework**

---

## [18:16](https://youtu.be/tUXJ2lTkoCs?t=1096) Environment Keywords Defined

An **environment keyword** describes the setting, pace, or structure of the workplace. It tells the recruiter what type of environment you perform well in.

**Examples of environment keywords:**
- Fast-paced, collaborative, agile, customer-focused
- Deadline-driven, hybrid, on-site, remote
- High-volume, startup, enterprise, cross-functional
- Mentorship-oriented, data-driven, product-led

These words help you **match your resume language** to the environment the employer highlights in the job description.

---

## [22:08](https://youtu.be/tUXJ2lTkoCs?t=1328) Anchor Keywords Across Job Descriptions

If you look at three different job descriptions for roles you want to apply to and you see keywords showing up **in all three**, these are your **anchor keywords**. They belong in:

- Your **skills section**
- Your **summary**
- Your **experience bullets** (where relevant)

---

## [23:18](https://youtu.be/tUXJ2lTkoCs?t=1398) Integrating Keywords Naturally

Keywords only help if they **read naturally**. ATS systems and recruiters ignore keyword dumping. Here is how to integrate keywords cleanly:

**In your summary:**
> Skilled in React, Node.js, REST API integration.

**In your skills section** (in order of importance):
> React, JavaScript, Node.js, SQL, Git, APIs

**In your experience bullets** (with context):
> Built REST API endpoints using Node.js and Express.

> Created test cases and automated regression tests using Jest.

**Match terminology exactly.** If the job description says "TypeScript," do not write "TS." Do not abbreviate.

**After:** Built and tested backend API routes using Node.js, Express, and MongoDB.

# Resume Clinic Week 5: Experience Bullets

Source: https://youtu.be/9X3j0cWOaWo
Date: November 25, 2025

---

## [5:52](https://youtu.be/9X3j0cWOaWo?t=352) Why Experience Bullets Matter

Your experience bullets are where recruiters look first and where you show your value. Recruiters **skim your resume** — they do not read every single bullet. They look for **short, clear statements that show outcomes**.

**Strong bullets share three qualities:**
1. They start with a **clear action verb**
2. They explain **what you worked on**
3. They show the **outcome or value** of your work

**Weak bullets list tasks. Strong bullets show why the task mattered.**

---

## [8:37](https://youtu.be/9X3j0cWOaWo?t=517) Weak vs Strong Bullet Examples

**Weak:** Responsible for helping with front-end updates.
**Strong:** Improved frontend layout by refactoring components for cleaner, more reusable code.

This is not about having five years of experience. This is about **taking credit** for what you have built, tested, fixed, or contributed to.

---

## [9:45](https://youtu.be/9X3j0cWOaWo?t=585) The Bullet Formula

Every experience bullet should follow this structure:

> **Action Verb + Task + Result**

If you have a metric, use it. If you do not have a metric, you can still show results using:
- **Clarity**
- **Scope**
- **Consistency**
- **Frequency**
- **Improvements**

---

## [10:40](https://youtu.be/9X3j0cWOaWo?t=640) Formula Examples

**With metrics:**
> Developed API endpoints using Node.js to support user profile management.

**Without metrics (using clarity):**
> Built React components that improved code readability and simplified feature updates.

**Using scope instead of data:**
> Collaborated with a team of four developers to design and deliver core project features.

**Using frequency:**
> Debugged multiple front-end issues weekly across different browsers and devices.

**Using improvement language:**
> Enhanced application stability by resolving API integration errors.

All five versions are valid and believable. **Impact does not always require numbers or metrics. It does require clarity.**

---

## [15:25](https://youtu.be/9X3j0cWOaWo?t=925) Writing Strong Bullets Without Metrics

Most early-career coders do not have production metrics. That is normal. You can still show impact using four methods:

### Scope — What Was the Scale of Your Work?
Size of the project, number of components, number of features, technologies used.
> Built three interactive pages using React, JSX, and API data integration.

### Consistency — How Often Did You Do This Task?
> Tested API responses daily using Postman to ensure consistent data validation.

### Quality or Improvement — Did Your Work Make Something Better?
Did your work make something cleaner, faster, clearer, or easier?
> Improved code readability by modularizing business logic across components.

### Collaboration and Delivery — How Did You Help the Team?
> Supported sprint delivery by completing assigned tickets and participating in code reviews.

These are the kinds of outcomes early-career recruiters expect.

When rewriting, you are not looking at your resume in its entirety. You are **breaking it down piece by piece** — one bullet at a time — so you can curate something meaningful in each segment.

If you are not proud of a project or not excited about it, **do not list it**. It is more impactful to see one or two exciting projects than a long list of mediocre ones.

## [38:26](https://youtu.be/9X3j0cWOaWo?t=2306) Showing Value in Bullets

Recruiters — especially tech recruiters — respond to **clarity, results, and ownership**.

When you look at your resume, ask yourself: **How am I showing value here?** Bullets are not about listing job functions. They are about showing **how you can impact business**.

For early-career developers: **focus on your resume first**. Get solid and confident in your resume, then move to GitHub (the industry standard for full-stack developers). A portfolio is a nice-to-have and can come later. Do not overwhelm yourself by tackling everything at once.

## [9:04](https://youtu.be/oCe2T7TUgDo?t=544) Why Numbers Matter on Your Resume

Recruiters scan resumes and their eyes go straight to **metrics**. Numbers make your work feel real. You do not need a corporate dashboard or performance report to use numbers. You can use:

- **Frequency**
- **Time saved**
- **User impact**

## [10:09](https://youtu.be/oCe2T7TUgDo?t=609) What Recruiters Want

## [17:15](https://youtu.be/oCe2T7TUgDo?t=1035) How to Estimate Numbers Honestly

Sometimes you do not have exact numbers. That is normal. Here is how to estimate honestly:

1. **Think about a normal week or sprint** — How many tickets, customers, pages, or tasks do you usually handle?
2. **Pick a range that feels realistic**

This phrasing is common and reasonable. It gives the recruiter a sense of scale **without pretending you tracked every single instance**.

**The rule:** Do not guess wildly. Think through your typical workload and choose a grounded range.

## [7:35](https://youtu.be/TJZbIcN18AM?t=455) Core Principle

Resume layout and design is one of the **fastest ways to improve your resume without rewriting any content**. A clean layout keeps recruiters reading. A cluttered layout makes them stop.

**The goal is not to make your resume look flashy.** The goal is to make your content:
- Easier to read
- Easier to trust
- Easier to remember

**Less is more.** Unless you are a designer, don't put design on your resume. Even if you are a designer—keep it simple.

---

## [8:56](https://youtu.be/TJZbIcN18AM?t=536) Critical Rule: No Photos on US Resumes

**Do not put your photo on your resume.** This is a European practice. In the US, don't do it.

If you want to include a photo, place it in your **online portfolio** in the About section—not on your resume, not on GitHub.

---

## [11:24](https://youtu.be/TJZbIcN18AM?t=684) How Recruiters Scan Resumes

Recruiters do not read resumes top to bottom—**they scan**.

**Typical scan pattern:**
1. Top third of the page
2. Section headers
3. Job titles and dates
4. First bullet or two under each role

Your layout either **supports the scan or fights it**. If a recruiter has to work to understand your resume, they move on.

---

## [12:19](https://youtu.be/TJZbIcN18AM?t=739) ATS Reality (Applicant Tracking Systems)

When you apply to a role (LinkedIn, Indeed, company career page), your application goes into an **Applicant Tracking System** (ATS). Common systems include:
- Ashby
- Greenhouse
- JobVite
- Workday

**ATS systems read line by line.** They struggle with:
- Tables
- Text boxes
- Graphics
- Complicated column layouts

Even if a resume looks nice to you, it may be **unreadable to software**.

## [13:42](https://youtu.be/TJZbIcN18AM?t=822) Core Design Rules

### [13:42](https://youtu.be/TJZbIcN18AM?t=822) Font
- **Use one font only**
- Stick to: Arial, Calibri, or Helvetica
- Body text: 10.5 to 12 point
- Section headers: slightly larger
- These fonts are readable, professional, and ATS-safe

### [14:04](https://youtu.be/TJZbIcN18AM?t=844) [approx] Color
- **Black or dark gray text only**
- No colored section headers
- No background shading
- Color distracts from content and breaks scanning patterns

### [14:48](https://youtu.be/TJZbIcN18AM?t=888) Formatting
- **Bold** for section headers and job titles
- Avoid italics for large blocks of text
- **No underlines** except for hyperlinks
- Make hyperlinks black (not blue) for ATS compliance

**Bold guides the eye. Colors confuse it.**

### [15:34](https://youtu.be/TJZbIcN18AM?t=934) Layout
- **Single column layout**
- Left-aligned text
- Consistent spacing between sections
- Symmetry builds trust
- Inconsistent spacing signals carelessness

## [21:07](https://youtu.be/TJZbIcN18AM?t=1267) [approx] Noisy vs Clean Layout

**Noisy Resume (avoid):**
- Two columns
- Icons for phone and email
- Skill bars
- Multiple fonts
- Tight spacing

**Clean Resume (goal):**
- One column
- Clear section headers
- Consistent bullet spacing
- Simple margins

**Which is easier to scan in 5 seconds?** The clean one.

The clean resume lets the content speak. The noisy resume forces the reader to decode the layout first.

**Design should disappear. Content should stand out.**

# Resume Clinic Week 10: Resume Customization for Job Postings

Source: https://youtu.be/5N7qDM_K8jM
Date: January 14, 2026

> **Note:** This session covers how to customize your resume for specific job postings without rewriting from scratch. Includes a live highlighting activity and participant discussion applying the concepts to real job postings.

---

## [8:13](https://youtu.be/5N7qDM_K8jM?t=493) Why Customization Matters

One resume does not work for every job. You can craft something that covers a range of roles, but customization is what makes you stand out in today's job market.

**As a recruiter, I can instantly tell who tailored their resume and who sent a generic one.** A generic resume looks disorganized, with a vague summary and skills that don't match the posting.

Customization drives three outcomes:

- **ATS rankings** -- Your resume scores higher in applicant tracking systems when keywords match
- **Recruiter attention** -- Matching language signals that you've read and understood the role
- **Interview selection** -- For specific positions, customization proves you're a targeted fit

**Job postings contain signals about skills, tools, and responsibilities.** Your resume must reflect those signals.

---

## [13:30](https://youtu.be/5N7qDM_K8jM?t=810) The Recruiter Scan

When a recruiter scans a resume for a specific job, they look for:

- **Matching keywords** from the job posting
- **Relevant projects or tech stack**
- **Evidence the candidate has done similar work**

If alignment isn't visible quickly, the recruiter moves on.

---

## [55:54](https://youtu.be/5N7qDM_K8jM?t=3354) 4 Core Customization Principles

### [55:54](https://youtu.be/5N7qDM_K8jM?t=3354) 1. Match Keywords and Phrasing from the Job Post

Pull keywords from these areas of the job description:

- **Required skills**
- **Preferred skills**
- **Responsibilities**
- **Tech stack**
- **Tools**
- **Methodology**

**Use the employer's language, not your own synonyms.** If the posting says "REST APIs," don't write "web services." Mirror their exact phrasing.

### [17:32](https://youtu.be/5N7qDM_K8jM?t=1052) 2. Highlight Projects and Bullets That Mirror the Role

If the role emphasizes API integration, testing, or SQL, surface those in your bullets and projects. Bring the most relevant work to the top.

### [18:02](https://youtu.be/5N7qDM_K8jM?t=1082) 3. Use a Core Skills Section for Flexibility

Include **8 to 12 job-relevant skills** that you can swap in and out based on each posting. This section is your fastest customization lever and also supports ATS keyword searches.

### [9:15](https://youtu.be/5N7qDM_K8jM?t=555) 4. Create a Usable Base Resume (Template)

Keep one master resume as your template. Make a copy for each application and only adjust:

- **Summary**
- **Core skills**
- **Relevant bullets**
- **Project ordering**

This keeps customization **fast and repeatable**. Using Google Drive makes this easy -- keep one master document and duplicate it for each application.

## [43:13](https://youtu.be/5N7qDM_K8jM?t=2593) 9 Common Customization Mistakes

1. **Applying without reading the full posting** -- This is a major mistake. Read the entire job description before applying.

2. **Copying the job description word for word** -- Recruiters wrote the posting. They recognize their own language pasted back at them.

3. **Listing irrelevant coursework or tools** -- If the posting doesn't ask for it, don't list it. Irrelevant skills can suggest misalignment or wasted time.

4. **Over-stuffing the skills section with keywords** -- Be strategic, not spammy. Quality over quantity.

5. **Ignoring required skill sections** -- If the posting lists a required skill, it must appear on your resume.

6. **Burying relevant projects at the bottom** -- Put your most relevant work first. Don't make the recruiter hunt for it.

7. **Using vague summaries that don't align with the job description** -- A generic summary signals a generic application.

8. **Applying with a 100% generic resume** -- No customization at all is the most common mistake.

9. **Forgetting to update your LinkedIn headline** -- If you're consistently applying to specific roles, make sure your LinkedIn headline reflects that focus.

- **Match employer language**
- **Surface relevant skills**
- **Prove you can do the work**
- **Raise your visibility** in ATS systems and recruiter scans

Every edit should increase alignment and raise your chances of being seen by the right recruiter. **This is how you make your resume work for you, not against you.**

## [15:33](https://youtu.be/Rd3heczszU8?t=933) What Cover Letters Are For

Cover letters are **not about length, repeating your resume, or sounding impressive**. They are about **relevance**.

In today's market, recruiters skim. Hiring managers skim. If your intro does not immediately show that you are a fit for the specific role, they will move on.

### [56:30](https://youtu.be/Rd3heczszU8?t=3390) When to Write a Cover Letter

1. Are you interested in the role, the company, or both?
2. What relevant experience do you bring?
3. What outcome can you help deliver?

## [19:15](https://youtu.be/Rd3heczszU8?t=1155) Cover Letter Structure: Three Short Paragraphs

### [19:15](https://youtu.be/Rd3heczszU8?t=1155) Paragraph 1: Why This Role

### [20:22](https://youtu.be/Rd3heczszU8?t=1222) Paragraph 2: What You Bring

- One skill area
- One example
- One outcome

### [32:36](https://youtu.be/Rd3heczszU8?t=1956) Paragraph 3: Close with Value and Intent

## [7:28](https://youtu.be/UkwJI-M2WrM?t=448) Core Principle

At this point your resume should not feel like a draft anymore. It should feel like a clear story about who you are, what you can do, and what kind of role you're targeting.

**If someone only reads the first third of your resume, do they understand your value?**

Recruiters don't read resumes line by line. They skim. The question this session answers is whether your resume communicates your value quickly enough to survive that skim.

---

## [9:42](https://youtu.be/UkwJI-M2WrM?t=582) How Recruiters Actually Review Resumes

When a recruiter opens your resume, they are doing **three things at once**:

1. **Scanning for fit** — Does this person match what I'm looking for?
2. **Checking for credibility** — Does this feel real and trustworthy?
3. **Looking for reasons to keep reading** — Is there enough here to move forward?

They are not trying to be critical. They are trying to make decisions quickly.

### [11:34](https://youtu.be/UkwJI-M2WrM?t=694) Recruiter Scan Order

Recruiters follow a predictable path through your resume:

1. **Name and headline**
2. **Summary**
3. **First role or project**
4. **Keywords and tools**
5. **Metrics and outcomes**

If your resume is clear, consistent, and aligned, it feels easy to read. If something is off even slightly, it creates friction — and **friction costs you attention**.

---

## [10:58](https://youtu.be/UkwJI-M2WrM?t=658) Resume Review Checklist: Four Lenses

Tonight's review examines your resume through four lenses:

1. **Clarity**
2. **Consistency**
3. **Keyword alignment**
4. **Proof of impact**

---

## [18:44](https://youtu.be/UkwJI-M2WrM?t=1124) Lens 1: Is It Easy to Skim?

Ask yourself these questions while looking at your resume through a recruiter's eyes:

- Can I understand this resume in **10 seconds**?
- Are sections **clearly labeled**?
- Is the spacing **consistent**?
- Are bullets **short and scannable**?

**If a resume feels dense, it doesn't get read deeply.** A recruiter who has to work to decode your layout will move on.

---

## [17:22](https://youtu.be/UkwJI-M2WrM?t=1042) [approx] Lens 2: Are Your Bullets Results-Driven?

Strong bullets answer three questions:

- **What did you do?**
- **How did you do it?**
- **Why did it matter?**

Weak bullets describe tasks. Strong bullets show **outcomes**. You may need more than two lines to show an outcome — that is acceptable if the content delivers value.

---

## [14:17](https://youtu.be/UkwJI-M2WrM?t=857) Lens 3: Do Titles and Summaries Match LinkedIn?

This is a big one that recruiters notice. Your resume and LinkedIn should **tell the same story**:

- Same **target role**
- Same **job titles**
- Same **language** around skills and tools

They shouldn't be identical word for word, but they should tell the same story. **If they don't match, it raises questions.**

---

## [15:28](https://youtu.be/UkwJI-M2WrM?t=928) Lens 4: Are Keywords Aligned to the Job Market?

Your resume should sound like the jobs you're applying for. If job postings mention React, APIs, testing, SQL, or Agile, those words should appear naturally in your resume.

**Keyword alignment is not stuffing — it's translation.** You are translating your experience into the language the market uses.

## [18:05](https://youtu.be/UkwJI-M2WrM?t=1085) Peer Review Activity

The peer review exercise is not about tearing resumes apart. It is about **helping each other see what recruiters see**.

### [18:22](https://youtu.be/UkwJI-M2WrM?t=1102) How to Run a Peer Review

1. Partner up and share your resumes
2. Use the four-lens checklist as your guide:
   - **Clarity:** Is it easy to skim? Can you understand it in 10 seconds?
   - **Results:** Are bullets results-driven? Do they answer what, how, and why it mattered?
   - **Consistency:** Do titles and summaries match LinkedIn?
   - **Keywords:** Are keywords aligned to the job market?
3. Give honest, constructive feedback focused on what recruiters would notice

# Resume Clinic Week 13: Resume and LinkedIn Alignment Audit

Source: https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM
Date: February 11, 2026

---

## [35:09](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=2109) Core Concept: Recruiters Never Evaluate Your Resume in Isolation

Your LinkedIn profile is part of the decision-making process. Recruiters use your resume and LinkedIn together, and they do it fast.

**If the two documents tell different stories, it raises immediate questions.**

---

## [35:12](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=2112) The Recruiter Workflow

When a recruiter receives your application, here is what typically happens:

1. **Open the resume** and scan for fit
2. **Check LinkedIn** to validate the narrative
3. **Compare signals** between the two documents
4. **Make a decision** — often in under 30 seconds

If there is consistency, confidence increases. If there are mismatches, questions appear. Recruiters do not like unanswered questions.

### [14:05](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=845) For Creative and Developer Roles

Recruiters hiring for creative or developer positions often check the portfolio or project work first, then LinkedIn, then the resume. The resume may come last, but it still needs to align with everything else.

---

## [13:49](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=829) What Mismatches Signal to Recruiters

When your resume and LinkedIn do not align, recruiters start asking:

- **Which title is accurate?**
- **Which role direction is real?**
- **Why are the skills different?**
- **Is this candidate unfocused, lazy, or clueless?**
- **Did they rush this application?**
- **Are they really qualified?**

Even small inconsistencies create uncertainty, and **uncertainty slows momentum**. Slower momentum means fewer callbacks.

### [14:08](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=848) Leaders Are Held to a Higher Standard

Senior-level candidates who never update their LinkedIn profile send a signal about how they manage their own professional presence. If a leadership candidate cannot keep their own profile current, it raises concerns about attention to detail.

---

## [15:19](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=919) Key Insight: Alignment Is Credibility, Not Perfection

It is not about making everything look identical. It is not about matching your LinkedIn profile and resume word for word.

**It is about presenting one clear, believable, intentional, professional identity.**

Your resume and LinkedIn should feel like **two views of the same person** — not two slightly different versions competing for attention.

When these two documents reinforce each other, recruiters trust the story faster and you look more believable.

---

## [24:49](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=1489) Managing Multiple Resume Versions

Having multiple versions of your resume is strategic. The key is knowing what stays stable and what can flex.

### [22:04](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=1324) What Must Stay Stable Across All Versions

- **Target role and professional direction** — Your positioning must feel intentional. Your identity should not shift dramatically between resumes.
- **Job titles** — Unless intentionally tailored, avoid rewriting your history.
- **Dates** — Never vary dates between resume versions. Even minor discrepancies trigger concern.

### [22:04](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=1324) [approx] Examples of Consistent vs. Inconsistent Titles

**Consistent:**
- Junior Front-End Developer
- Junior Front-End Developer, React Focus
- Junior Front-End Developer, UI and Accessibility

**Inconsistent:**
- Junior Developer
- QA Tester
- Data Analyst

**Safe title variation:** QA Engineer / Quality Assurance Engineer

**Risky title variation:** QA Engineer / Software Developer / Product Manager

### [24:26](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=1466) What Can Flex Between Versions

These elements are designed to be tailored per application:

- **Summary wording**
- **Bullet order**
- **Highlighted projects**
- **Skill emphasis**
- **Keywords**

For example, you might adjust emphasis across versions:

- **Front-end role:** React, UI, CSS, performance
- **Backend role:** APIs, Node.js, databases
- **QA role:** Testing, automation, defect tracking

This is **strategic alignment**, not inconsistency.

---

## [25:21](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=1521) LinkedIn as Your Umbrella Narrative

**Your LinkedIn should act as your umbrella narrative. It should support your overall direction, not mirror every tailored resume.**

Your LinkedIn profile should be broad but aligned. It reflects your main direction while supporting all versions of your resume.

### [26:12](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=1572) How It Works in Practice

- **LinkedIn headline:** "Junior Front-End Developer | React | JavaScript"
- **Resume Version A:** Emphasizes UI / React
- **Resume Version B:** Emphasizes APIs / Backend

Both versions align under the same professional identity that LinkedIn establishes.

### [33:06](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=1986) [approx] Where Candidates Get Into Trouble

The problem occurs when:

- The resume says **Developer** and LinkedIn says **QA**
- The resume says **Front End** and LinkedIn says **Data Analyst**
- The resume says **Engineer** and LinkedIn says **Career Changer**
- The resume says **Full Stack Developer** and LinkedIn says **Aspiring Engineer, Open to Anything**

**The golden rule:** You can tailor your resume for different roles. You should not change who you are at your core.

### [27:59](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=1679) Practical Structure

1. **Base resume (master version):** Complete history, all projects, all skills
2. **Targeted versions:** Front-end version, backend version, QA/SDET version
3. **Stable LinkedIn profile:** Broad but aligned, reflects main direction, supports all versions

**Multiple resumes signal strategy. Misaligned branding signals confusion.**

---

## [38:37](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=2317) [approx] Non-Negotiables: What Must Match Between Resume and LinkedIn

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| **Headline and target role** | Same role name, same direction, same seniority level |
| **Role titles** | Titles must match on both platforms |
| **Summary / About section** | Same positioning, same core skills, same tone and intent |

These elements do not need to be word-for-word identical, but they **must sound like the same person**.

---

## [38:43](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=2323) Side Projects: How to Frame Them

If you list a side project on LinkedIn (especially as a founder or co-founder), be mindful of how it reads to recruiters. Too much enthusiasm for a side project can raise concerns that it will take focus away from a full-time role.

**Frame side projects as:**
- A passion project born from genuine interest
- A way to build skills and help others
- Something that demonstrates initiative, not a competing business

How you talk about it and frame it determines whether it makes you look like someone anyone wants on the team or someone who might be distracted.

---

## [38:41](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=2321) Live Audit Exercise: Comparing Resume and LinkedIn Side by Side

Open your resume and LinkedIn profile side by side and work through each section.

### [24:16](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=1456) What to Check

1. **Headline vs. resume summary** — Are they pointing in the same direction?
2. **Job titles** — Do they match across both platforms?
3. **Skills list** — Are the same skills present on both? In a logical order?
4. **Projects** — Are they consistent?
5. **Keywords** — Do they align?

### [45:10](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=2710) Findings from the Live Audit

Comparing the two documents revealed common misalignment patterns:

- **Skill ordering differences** — Skills listed in different priority order on each platform
- **Missing entries** — Items on LinkedIn not present on the resume (and vice versa)
- **Title variations** — Small differences like "Software Engineering Intern" vs. "Software Engineer Intern"
- **Extra items on one platform** — LinkedIn showing roles (e.g., portrait photographer, founder) not on the resume
- **Outdated skills** — Skills on LinkedIn that no longer reflect current direction (e.g., outdated technologies from 15 years ago)

### [52:23](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=3143) Key Takeaway from the Audit

**If you are not listing something on your resume, do not list it on LinkedIn.** Make sure the skills on both your resume and LinkedIn align. Anything outside of those shared skills should be removed or added to both platforms.

---

## [45:36](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=2736) Recommendations on LinkedIn = Credibility Reinforcement

Recommendations on LinkedIn support your resume claims. They do not need to be perfect — they just need to be relevant.

- **The resume makes the claim**
- **LinkedIn reinforces it** through recommendations from colleagues and managers

---

## [35:43](https://youtu.be/Ppt-waVluMM?t=2143) Session Takeaways

- **Alignment builds trust** between your resume and LinkedIn
- **Small fixes have big impact** on how recruiters perceive your consistency
- When your resume and LinkedIn **speak with one voice**, recruiters stop questioning and start engaging
- Start from the top down, just like reviewing a resume — do not get overwhelmed
- Use AI tools to quickly scan and compare skills lists across both platforms

# Resume Clinic Week 14: Job Application Strategy (Final Session)

Source: https://youtu.be/E_dMvNVcI88
Date: February 18, 2026

> **Note:** This is the final session of Chelsea's Resume Clinic series. The focus shifts from resume building to putting your assets to work through a systematic job application process. Chelsea also announces her departure from TechJoy.

---

## [5:50](https://youtu.be/E_dMvNVcI88?t=350) Core Principle: Momentum Over Perfection

A great resume without consistent action produces nothing. You have spent weeks building assets — resume, LinkedIn, projects, cover letters. Now you build the system that puts those assets to work.

**Preparation creates readiness. Action creates opportunity.**

---

## [11:52](https://youtu.be/E_dMvNVcI88?t=712) Warning: AI Application Platforms

**Do not use AI platforms that apply to jobs on your behalf.** Recruiters can tell when these platforms are used, and the consequences are serious:

- Applications look **fraudulent** — the email address often routes through a third party
- Recruiters **cannot reach you** because the contact information doesn't connect to your real inbox
- It signals to recruiters that you are **taking the easy road** and not putting in the work
- Many recruiters will **skip your application entirely** without review

**The right way to use AI:** Draft cover letters, customize resumes, prepare materials — but **you fill out the application yourself**, use your real email address, and proofread everything before sending.

---

## [12:49](https://youtu.be/E_dMvNVcI88?t=769) Hiring Timelines: Why Speed Matters

Most candidates misunderstand hiring timelines. Recruiters often review applications within the **first 24 to 72 hours** before hundreds of applicants accumulate.

**Early candidates receive disproportionate attention** because:
- Smaller applicant pool
- Less screening fatigue
- Faster recruiter response cycles

**The rule: Apply within 24 to 48 hours of a posting going live.** Not because later is impossible — because earlier is advantageous.

Popular roles can receive **100 to 500 applicants** very quickly. **Speed plus alignment wins.**

---

## [13:59](https://youtu.be/E_dMvNVcI88?t=839) Four Pillars of Job Application

### [13:59](https://youtu.be/E_dMvNVcI88?t=839) 1. Application Speed

Apply within **24 to 48 hours** of a posting going live.

**LinkedIn tip:** Set job alerts filtered to show postings from **1 hour to 24 hours old**. Use both **LinkedIn and Indeed** to search — not every organization posts on both platforms. Some companies skip LinkedIn because a recruiter seat costs several thousand dollars per year.

### [19:00](https://youtu.be/E_dMvNVcI88?t=1140) 2. Application Tracking

**Your memory is unreliable. Systems are not.**

Use a spreadsheet (Google Sheets, Excel, or a project management tool like Asana) with these required columns:

| Column | Why It Matters |
|--------|---------------|
| **Company** | Provides context |
| **Role** | Positioning |
| **Date Applied** | Timing and follow-up logic |
| **Contact Name** | Person who posted the job |
| **LinkedIn Outreach** | Names you reached out to |
| **Contact Method** | LinkedIn, referral, email |
| **Referral Source** | If someone referred you |
| **Status** | Reviewed / Applied / Heard Back — pipeline visibility |
| **Follow-up Date** | When you followed up |
| **Notes** | Any additional context |

**What gets tracked gets executed. Simplicity over complexity.**

### [21:05](https://youtu.be/E_dMvNVcI88?t=1265) 3. Follow-up Strategy

**The #1 thing to do after applying:** Find a connection at the company.

**Search priority order:**
1. Check if a recruiter or employee is attached to the job posting (LinkedIn and Indeed often show this)
2. Search the company on LinkedIn for **Talent Acquisition** or **Recruiter** titles
3. If none found, look for **HR Generalist** or **HR Partner** — they are likely handling sourcing
4. Then look for **leaders within the department** you are targeting

Once you identify someone, **send a connection request with a note** or a LinkedIn message.

**Alumni connections** can help but should not be your first priority — focus on people who are actually doing the hiring.

### [46:03](https://youtu.be/E_dMvNVcI88?t=2763) 4. Weekly Activity Targets

**Consistency beats intensity.** Set weekly targets:

- **5** quality applications
- **2** networking messages
- **1** follow-up on a previous application
- **1** resume customization per role

Small weekly actions compound into opportunity.

---

## [42:24](https://youtu.be/E_dMvNVcI88?t=2544) Follow-up Template

Send a follow-up **within 3 to 7 days** after applying. Following up is not annoying to a recruiter if done correctly — it signals interest, initiative, and professionalism.

**Template:**

> I recently applied for the [position] and wanted to briefly introduce myself. My experience with [relevant skill/area] aligns closely with the role's focus on [specific responsibility]. I'd welcome the opportunity to contribute and would be happy to provide any additional information.

**Why this works:** It is respectful, specific, concise, and applies no pressure.

**Tip:** Save follow-up templates in your email platform (Outlook signatures, Gmail templates) so you can quickly customize and send them.

## [50:56](https://youtu.be/E_dMvNVcI88?t=3056) [approx] Series Wrap-Up: What You Should Have

By the end of this 14-week clinic, you should have:

- **One recruiter-ready resume** that serves as your base template
- **One short cover letter template** ready to customize
- **An active job tracker** (spreadsheet or project management tool)
- **A resume/LinkedIn alignment checklist** verified and consistent

**You are no longer preparing every time. You are now operating.**

Systems turn effort into momentum. Momentum turns conversations into offers.
