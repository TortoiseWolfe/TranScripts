# Resume Clinic Week 2: Structure and Format
Source: https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ

---

## [7:30](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=450) Session Overview

Tonight's focus is on **structure and format** — if a recruiter can't navigate your resume in six seconds, they won't. The goal is to understand the difference between resume structures, when to use each, and how formatting impacts a recruiter's readability.

Recruiters scan resumes within **6 to 10 seconds**. They look for:

- **Clarity**
- **Structure**
- **Alignment with the role**

The format should **guide their eyes, not overwhelm them**. There needs to be cohesive design sensibility — consistent font size, simple font choice, clean layout.

---

## [8:13](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=493) Two Core Resume Structures

### Reverse Chronological

- **Most common and preferred** for consistent career paths
- Best when you're staying in the same field you've been working in
- Highlights **steady progression** and recent experience
- Easy for recruiters and **applicant tracking systems (ATS)** to read

### Functional (Skills-Based)

- **Ideal for career changers** or those with mixed experience
- Groups experience **under skill categories instead of dates**
- Highlights **transferable skills and impact** instead of job titles
- Chelsea recommends this format for most TechJoy students since the majority are career shifters

---

## [16:30](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=990) When to Use Each Format

| Use Reverse Chronological When... | Use Functional When... |
|---|---|
| Staying in the same industry | **Pivoting careers** (e.g., teacher becoming developer) |
| Consistent experience and titles | Gaps or **mixed experiences** |
| Want to showcase steady growth | Need to highlight **skills before timeline** |

The functional resume works well for people with **multiple talents** that complement each other. A recruiter seeing diverse but related experience (e.g., lighting design + web development) gets insight into who you are and what your path looks like.

## [23:00](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=1380) Resume Anatomy: Non-Negotiable Sections

Every resume must include these sections:

1. **Header** — Name, LinkedIn profile link, contact information, GitHub link (for developers), portfolio link (if you have produced work you have permission to highlight)
2. **Summary** — Two to three sentences minimum; can go longer if warranted
3. **Core Skills / Technical Stack** — 100% non-negotiable
4. **Experience or Projects** — Must be present
5. **Education and Certifications** — List all certifications

## [25:12](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=1512) Formatting Rules

- **Keep margins balanced** and fonts consistent
- Font size can vary slightly — name and header elements can be a bit larger than body text
- **No photographs** on your resume (standard in Europe but not in the US; it's a distraction)
- **No design-heavy templates** — graphics, multiple colors, and decorative elements distract ATS systems and the AI that parses skills and competencies
- **Stick to one page** unless you have extensive relevant experience; two pages is acceptable, three is for very experienced professionals
- **Keep it simple** to ensure ATS systems read your resume correctly

---

## [26:50](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=1610) Skills Section Formatting

Two acceptable approaches for listing skills:

- **Bulleted list** of individual skills
- **Grouped by category** under the type of skill or technology

Either format works — choose what makes sense for the volume and type of skills you have. If you have many valuable skills, organizing them into columns or categories can keep things clean and readable.

## [30:00](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=1800) Live Resume Edit: Condensing the Header

Chelsea demonstrated live edits on a student resume in Google Docs. Key changes:

### Header Condensation

**Before:** Full URLs written out for LinkedIn, Portfolio, and GitHub
**After:** Hyperlinked text — just the word "LinkedIn," "Portfolio," or "GitHub" each linking to the URL

This saves significant space and looks cleaner.

### Headline Placement

The target role headline (e.g., "WordPress Developer") goes **directly underneath your name**, before contact information and summary.

### Spacing and Formatting

- Set line spacing to **single**
- Use **custom spacing** (before/after paragraph) set to approximately 3pt for consistency
- Only bold **section headers**, not body text
- Use consistent indentation for bullet points
- Move bullet points to align with the left margin using the ruler

These formatting adjustments **significantly reduced the resume's page count** without removing any content.

## [32:28](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=1948) Hyperlinks in Resumes

**Hyperlink your profile links** instead of writing out full URLs:

- The word "**LinkedIn**" hyperlinked to your LinkedIn profile
- The word "**Portfolio**" hyperlinked to your portfolio site
- The word "**GitHub**" hyperlinked to your GitHub profile

This keeps the header compact and professional.

## [46:30](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=2790) Using Google Docs for Resume Management

**Template workflow for tailoring resumes:**

1. Keep your **master resume** as a Google Doc template
2. When applying to a specific job, go to **File > Make a Copy**
3. Edit the copy to tailor it for that application
4. **Always keep the original template** untouched for reference

### Exporting for Applications

- **Download as PDF** when ready to submit
- **Never apply with a Word document** — it can be altered
- Staffing firms have been known to remove candidate names from Word documents, add their own branding, and submit candidates for jobs without permission

## [53:30](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=3210) Resume as a Living Document

Your resume is a **living, breathing story** of your professional career. It will constantly evolve, especially in tech where frameworks, databases, and tools change rapidly. Skills that are essential today may be obsolete in a year or two.

A clear, focused format shows recruiters you **respect their time** — it's the first sign you're ready to be hired.

## [55:35](https://youtu.be/uLGKKcpM9gQ?t=3335) Homework Assignment

1. **Pick one structure** — reverse chronological or functional — and rebuild your base layout
2. **Create a one-page draft layout** (or two pages if needed; the "one page only" rule is a myth)
3. Add your **header, summary, and skills section** using the formatting guidelines from this session
4. If you don't have content ready, **use placeholder text** — the focus is on format, not content yet
5. Bring your draft to the next Q&A for **peer and coach review**
