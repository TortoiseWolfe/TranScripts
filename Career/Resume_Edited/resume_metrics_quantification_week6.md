## [9:04](https://youtu.be/oCe2T7TUgDo?t=544) Why Numbers Matter on Your Resume

Recruiters scan resumes and their eyes go straight to **metrics**. Numbers make your work feel real. You do not need a corporate dashboard or performance report to use numbers. You can use:

- **Counts**
- **Frequency**
- **Time saved**
- **User impact**
- **Estimates** (as long as you stay honest)

---

## [10:09](https://youtu.be/oCe2T7TUgDo?t=609) What Recruiters Want

When recruiters read your resume, they want two things fast: **what you did** and **how much it mattered**. Numbers give a quick picture of impact. Adjectives do not.

**Before:** Responsible for testing features.
**After:** Tested three features per sprint and logged bugs before release.

**Before:** Helped customers with questions.
**After:** Supported 20-30 customer requests each week by email and chat.

Same work, completely different impact.

## [11:49](https://youtu.be/oCe2T7TUgDo?t=709) Why Recruiters Scan for Metrics

Numbers show three things:
1. **Scale** — how much you handled
2. **Consistency** — how reliably you delivered
3. **Trust and responsibility** — what was entrusted to you

Your job is to **tell the truth and make the scale visible**.

## [13:06](https://youtu.be/oCe2T7TUgDo?t=786) What You Can Measure in Non-Numeric Roles

Many people feel stuck because they think "I do not have sales numbers" or "my projects are in school or a boot camp." You still have options:

- **Frequency** — How often did you do something?
- **Volume** — How many items, users, tickets, pages, or features?
- **Time** — How long something took or how much time you helped save?
- **Scope** — Size of the project or team?
- **Reach** — Number of people who used what you built or supported?

## [17:15](https://youtu.be/oCe2T7TUgDo?t=1035) How to Estimate Numbers Honestly

Sometimes you do not have exact numbers. That is normal. Here is how to estimate honestly:

1. **Think about a normal week or sprint** — How many tickets, customers, pages, or tasks do you usually handle?
2. **Pick a range that feels realistic**
3. **Use language that signals an estimate:** "about," "around," "roughly," "X to Y"

**Examples with estimate language:**
- Supported roughly 15-20 customer requests per week.
- Reviewed 2-3 pull requests per sprint.
- Tested around five key user flows per release.

This phrasing is common and reasonable. It gives the recruiter a sense of scale **without pretending you tracked every single instance**.

**The rule:** Do not guess wildly. Think through your typical workload and choose a grounded range.

## [20:13](https://youtu.be/oCe2T7TUgDo?t=1213) Activity: Brainstorm Your Metrics

Write down **three raw metrics** from your experience (current or past projects):

1. About how many **features, pages, or components** have you built?
2. About how many **users, tickets, or customers** have you supported?
3. About how many **projects, sprints, or group assignments** have you completed?

Do not overthink. Write quick, honest guesses.

## [25:05](https://youtu.be/oCe2T7TUgDo?t=1505) Apply the Formula with Templates

Take each metric and wrap it in a sentence using the **Action Verb + Task + Result** formula.

### Template 1
> **Action verb** + what you worked on + for how many/how often + result or purpose.

### Template 2
> **Action verb** + number + items/features/tickets + in time frame + which resulted in/supported + outcome.

### Template 3
> **Action verb** + task + handling volume or frequency + to support team/users/project.

## [45:24](https://youtu.be/oCe2T7TUgDo?t=2724) Quick Fixes for Weak Bullets

When reviewing your resume, apply these upgrades:

- Replace **"worked on"** with **"built," "implemented,"** or **"designed"**
- **Name the tech stack** used
- **Clarify who benefited** — user, team, customer, instructor

Ask yourself:
- Which bullets can gain a **number**?
- Which bullets can gain a **frequency or range**?
- Which bullets can gain a **time or speed element**?

Pick two existing bullets and rewrite them with **one action verb** from the list, plus **one metric, range, or time reference**.

## Key Takeaway

Numbers tell your story faster than any adjective. Recruiters scan for metrics because metrics show **scale and trust**. You do not need perfect data — you need **honest, reasonable numbers** that bring your work to life. Data sells competence. Use numbers to make your results undeniable.
